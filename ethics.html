Ethical Lenses

To analyze wearable health trackers such as the Apple Watch and Fitbit, our group is using two ethical lenses: Utilitarianism and Deontology / Duty Ethics. These lenses help us understand both the benefits and risks of wearable health technology.

Utilitarianism

Utilitarianism focuses on whether a technology creates the greatest overall benefit for the greatest number of people. From this perspective, wearable health trackers can be seen as beneficial because they help users monitor their health, promote physical activity, identify possible health concerns, and support preventative healthcare.

Devices like the Apple Watch and Fitbit can encourage healthier habits by tracking steps, sleep, heart rate, and physical activity. The Apple Watch can also alert users about irregular heart rhythms, while Fitbit can help users stay aware of their daily activity and wellness patterns. Under this ethical lens, wearable health trackers seem more acceptable because they have the potential to improve public health and help people make better lifestyle choices.

Deontology / Duty Ethics

Deontology, also known as duty ethics, focuses on rights, responsibilities, privacy, and consent. From this perspective, wearable health trackers become more ethically questionable because the main concern is not only whether the device helps people, but whether it respects their rights.

These devices collect sensitive personal information such as heart rate, sleep habits, activity levels, and sometimes location data. If users do not fully understand how their data is collected, stored, or shared, then their privacy and consent may not be properly respected. Even if the technology provides benefits, companies still have a duty to protect user data, be transparent, and avoid misleading users into thinking the device is always medically accurate.

How the Lenses Change Our Understanding

Under utilitarianism, wearable health trackers appear more positive because they can improve health awareness, encourage exercise, and help users detect possible health issues earlier. If many people use these devices to improve their health, the overall social benefit could be significant.

Under deontology and duty ethics, the technology appears more harmful or questionable because it collects sensitive health data. Companies have an ethical responsibility to protect user privacy, receive clear permission, and avoid using or sharing data in ways that users would not expect. This lens also raises concerns when users rely too heavily on wearable devices instead of seeking professional medical advice.

Ethical Conflicts

The main ethical conflict is between health benefits and individual rights. Utilitarianism may support wearable health trackers because they can improve health outcomes for many people. However, duty ethics reminds us that positive outcomes do not excuse weak privacy protections, unclear data policies, or lack of informed consent.

Another conflict is between convenience and responsibility. Apple Watch and Fitbit make health tracking easier, but users may misread the data, become anxious about their health statistics, or treat the device like a doctor. This shows that wearable health trackers can be useful, but they must be designed and used responsibly.

Overall Ethical View

Overall, wearable health trackers are ethically complicated because they can support better health and prevention while also creating risks related to privacy, consent, accuracy, and over-reliance. These devices are not simply good or bad. Their ethical value depends on how they are designed, how companies handle personal data, and how users understand the limits of the technology.
